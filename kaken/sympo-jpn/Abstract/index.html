Title

Between Rai na'in and Rai Timor: Nationalism and Belonging at scale in Timor Leste.

Andrew McWilliam Western Sydney University E: [a.mcwilliam@westernsydney.edu.au]

Abstract (300characters)

Historically the diverse ethno-linguistic communities of Timor-Leste have defined themselves through local resource jurisdictions of belonging (Rai na'in). But the independence struggle forged novel, imaginative connections to the idea of a national homeland (Rai Timor). This paper examines the relationship and latent tension between these two encompassing ontologies.

Abstract (250 words)

Historically the diverse ethno-linguistic communities of Timor-Leste have long defined themselves through local ancestral resource jurisdictions and mythic histories of settlement, exchange and alliance. Central to this conception of place and belonging is the idea of the Rai na'in, a Tetum language term with local language variants that refer to ‘custodians of the land'. The brutal, generation long struggle for independence however, promoted new forms of imaginative connection and belonging encapsulated in the concept of Rai Timor or homeland. The notion of Rai Timor is not merely a more encompassing ‘homeland' than the landed inheritance of locally embedded communities, but it is imagined in a different way, as a territory shaped from below and collectively, by the ordeals of ‘the people', who become the active originators of the nation [Traube 2007] . If the constitutive act of a subject in the traditional ideology of rule is to recognise and defer to authority vested in ritual and political leaders (the rai na'in), the constitutive act of belonging to the nation is to suffer and sacrifice for it (the Rai Timor) [McWilliam and Traube 2011]. This presentation considers the contemporary force of this expansive sense of the imagined community in Timor-Leste - a notion that Anderson also described as ‘aggregated nativeness'(2003) - in the light of the well documented resurgence of custom (kultura) and traditional authority. How do these different scales of allegiance and belonging contribute to the shaping of contemporary society in post-independence Timor-Leste? I discuss these and other questions with reference to the Fataluku ethnography.
